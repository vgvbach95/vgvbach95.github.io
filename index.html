To Be Creative

There is no greater satisfaction than bringing something to life, whether it be in engineering or in art.

I am a crafter. I love creating costumes and outfits that would otherwise exist on a page or a screen. Halloween is my favorite holiday, and I have designed and built haunted houses for charity during my Freshman and Sophmore years of college. My ongoing quest is to merge tech into my creations.

I also draw and do graphic design when needed, with much of my published work being conference media for Reflections|Projections by ACM@UIUC.

Hatsune Miku

This outfit was my first encounter with a large sewing project. I did the entire outfit from scratch in just a month and debuted it at Anime Midwest 2015.

This was also my first time using EL-Wire. I stitched the wire into the tie and fashioned it to my legs to add a more electronic sense to the outfit.

All photography credit goes to Pritan Vora.

The character is Vocaloid Hatsune Miku, and the outfit is from the 2015 Magical Mirai holographic concert in Japan. My work is purely for my own enjoyment, and I do not own the character nor am affiliated with Crypton Media.

Jean Kirstein

While I did not make the outfit, I made the 3DMG. The gear was entirely built out of cardboard, and was clumsy, but close to the right size. My main task in it was to design blades that could be removed from the handles and swapped out, just in case one got damaged during a convention.

Code With A Passion

I have been waist deep in code ever since I unknowningly volunteered to be on the programming team for a high school robotics club.

Since then, I have experimented with many applications of Computer Science. My focus is Machine Learning and Artificial Intelligence with a special niche for robotics. Through internships, classes, and personal projects, I have dabbled in everything from wearable electronics to front end and back end web development to data visualization to virtual reality to automated testing. I love expanding my boundaries and discovering new ways to bring this hodgepodge set of skills together.

VR Horror

Virtual Reality Project: Simulate human eye adaptation differences in light levels

Sample Horror Game with scotopic and photopic vision experiment for the Oculus Rift. Requires Unity Pro 4 to modify.

Authors: Victoria Vanderbach and Jessica Spratley

Goal: Imitate the human eye adjustments between low light vision and day time vision in a video game setting on the Oculus Rift.

Background:

Ever walked into a dark room and couldn't see at first, but over time, you begin to be able to see despite the low light?

The human eye has mechanisms to help you see in any amount of light. We wanted to simulate this temporary blinding sensation and the transitions in virtual reality to add a level of comfort to the VR experience.
